Know Your Windows

Your windows are one of the most important features of your home — they impact comfort, efficiency, and appearance every day.

This guide is designed to help you better understand the key parts and functions of your windows, and what to expect from their performance.

Whether you're a new homeowner or simply looking to learn more, "Know Your Windows" will give you a clear picture of how your windows work and to also give you better understanding on what you are looking for.

Double Hung

Definition

A double-hung window is a type of window made up of two separate sashes — one on the top and one on the bottom — that slide vertically within the frame. Both sashes move independently, meaning you can open either the top, the bottom, or both at the same time.

Features

Two Movable Sashes:
Each sash operates on its own track, allowing flexible ventilation. You can open the bottom sash to let fresh air in, or open the top sash to release warm air.

Tilt-in design:
Both sashes can tilt inward, making it easy to clean the exterior glass from inside your home without using a ladder.

Exterior screen:
The screen is placed on the outside of the window, preventing insects from entering while the window is open.

Block and tackle balance system:
Inside the frame, a system of cords and pulleys helps the sashes move smoothly up and down. This system is built for long-term use and can withstand thousands of open-and-close cycles before any maintenance is needed.

Advantages

Flexible ventilation:
Double-hung windows allow you to control airflow naturally. Opening both sashes lets warm air escape through the top and cool air enter through the bottom, improving comfort and air circulation.

Easy maintenance:
The tilt-in feature makes cleaning quick and safe, since both sides of the glass are accessible from inside the home.

Energy efficiency:
Modern double-hung windows are designed with insulated glass and advanced weather sealing, helping to reduce drafts and improve energy performance year-round.

Classic appearance:
Their traditional design complements most home styles, from historic to contemporary, adding a timeless look to the exterior.

Common Uses

Residential homes:
Double-hung windows are among the most popular styles in homes because they combine beauty, function, and convenience.

Limited exterior space:
Because the sashes move vertically rather than swinging outward, these windows are ideal for areas where space outside is limited — such as near walkways, porches, or patios.





Casement Windows

Definition

Casement windows are hinged windows that open outward like a door. They are typically operated by a crank or lever mechanism, allowing easy and precise control over how far the window opens.

Features

Hinged Design:
Casement windows are hinged on one side, enabling them to swing open either to the left or right.

Crank or Lever Operation:
Operated by a crank or lever, these windows allow smooth and adjustable opening and closing.

Screen Placement:
Screens are generally installed on the inside to protect them from weather exposure and to keep insects out when the window is open.

Blinds Between the Glass:
Casement windows can be customized with integrated blinds positioned between the glass panes. This feature provides a clean, low-maintenance appearance and allows for easy light control. (Note: blinds between the glass cannot be combined with decorative grids.)

Advantages

Unobstructed Views:
Casement windows feature large, uninterrupted glass panels that enhance natural light and provide clear outdoor visibility.

Energy Efficiency:
The sash presses firmly against the frame when closed, creating a tight seal that minimizes air leakage and improves thermal performance.

Superior Ventilation:
When opened, the sash catches and directs breezes into the home, maximizing airflow.

Enhanced Security:
Casement windows often include hook-shaped locks embedded into the frame, making them highly resistant to forced entry.

Modern Customization Options:
Integrated blinds between the glass offer a neat, contemporary appearance and are easy to operate and maintain.

Common Uses

Residential Homes:
Widely used in homes for their combination of ventilation, visibility, and energy efficiency.

Modern and Contemporary Architecture:
Their clean lines and functional design complement modern architectural aesthetics.

Rooms Requiring Controlled Ventilation:
Ideal for areas such as kitchens, bathrooms, or any space where precise airflow control is desired.





Hopper Windows

Definition

Hopper windows are small, hinged windows that open inward from the top. They are typically used in basements, bathrooms, or other confined areas where ventilation and natural light are needed.

Features

Bottom-Hinged Design:
Hopper windows are hinged at the bottom, allowing them to open inward from the top.

Compact Size:
Their smaller dimensions make them ideal for spaces with limited room.

Locking Mechanism:
They feature secure locks that keep the window tightly closed when not in use.

Screen Placement:
Screens are positioned on the exterior side to prevent insects from entering when the window is open.

Advantages

Efficient Ventilation:
Hopper windows provide strong air circulation, making them great for humid or enclosed spaces.

Enhanced Security:
Their inward-opening design and locking system make them hard to force open from the outside.

Energy Efficiency:
When closed, they form a tight seal that minimizes drafts and improves insulation.

Easy Maintenance:
The inward-opening motion allows easy cleaning of both interior and exterior glass surfaces.

Common Uses

Basements:
Commonly used to allow fresh air and light in underground or partially below-ground rooms.

Bathrooms:
Provide ventilation and moisture control while maintaining privacy.

Compact Spaces:
Perfect for tight or narrow wall openings where standard windows won't fit.





Double-Slider Windows

Definition

Double-slider windows feature two horizontally sliding sashes that move side-to-side within a single frame. Both sashes can operate independently, providing flexibility for airflow and ease of use.

Features

Two Sliding Sashes:
Both panels glide horizontally, allowing ventilation from either side.

Effortless Operation:
Designed for smooth and easy opening and closing with minimal force.

Removable Sashes:
Each sash can often be lifted out of the frame for convenient cleaning and maintenance.

Exterior Screen:
A full-width screen is typically mounted on the outside to keep insects out while maximizing airflow.

Advantages

Wide Openings:
Double-slider windows offer broad, unobstructed openings ideal for maximum ventilation and emergency egress.

Balanced Ventilation:
Both sashes can be adjusted to control airflow from either side for optimal comfort.

Low Maintenance:
With fewer moving parts compared to crank or hinged windows, these units require minimal upkeep.

Space-Saving Design:
Because the sashes slide within the frame, they do not protrude outward, making them ideal for walkways, or tight exterior spaces.

Common Uses

Residential Applications:
Commonly installed in living rooms, bedrooms, and basements for their practicality and balanced ventilation.

Homes with Limited Exterior Clearance:
Perfect for spaces where traditional outward-opening windows are impractical.





End-Vent Slider Windows

Definition

End-vent slider windows are a type of horizontal sliding window that feature a fixed center pane and two operable side panes. This design allows for both a wide, unobstructed view and flexible ventilation from either side.

Features

Fixed Center Pane:
The large middle section remains stationary, providing a clear, uninterrupted view.

Sliding Side Panes:
The left and right panels slide horizontally to allow controlled airflow.

Low-Profile Design:
The frame offers a modern, streamlined appearance suitable for contemporary homes.

1/3 | 1/3 | 1/3 Configuration:
Three equal panels—one fixed in the center and two operable on each side—create a balanced, symmetrical look.

1/4 | 2/4 | 1/4 Configuration:
The wider center panel and narrower side sliders offer a panoramic view with efficient ventilation.

Exterior Screen Placement:
Screens are positioned on the outside of the sliding panels to prevent insects from entering while maintaining full functionality.

Advantages

Expansive Ventilation:
Both side sashes can open to provide generous airflow across the room.

Clear Visibility:
The center fixed panel delivers a wide, unobstructed view of the outdoors.

Ideal for Large Openings:
Perfect for spaces too wide for traditional double sliders, maintaining structural integrity and aesthetics.

Smooth Operation:
The sliding panels glide easily for effortless opening and closing.

Common Uses

Residential Spaces:
Commonly used in living rooms, family rooms, and sunrooms where homeowners want both wide views and functional ventilation.

Modern Architecture:
The sleek design complements wide wall openings and contemporary home styles.





Garden Windows

Definition

Garden windows are three-dimensional, box-shaped windows that extend outward from a home's exterior wall. They create a mini-greenhouse effect, offering space to grow plants, display decorations, or simply enjoy increased natural light.

Features

Box-Shaped Design:
Projects outward from the wall, creating an enclosed area that adds depth and dimension to the room.

Multiple Panes:
Includes a large fixed center pane, angled side panes, and a sloped top pane to maximize sunlight exposure.

Ventilation:
Often designed with operable casement-style side panels for airflow and temperature control.

Built-in Shelves:
Many models include glass or insulated shelves for displaying plants or decorative pieces.

Insulated Seatboards:
The base (or "seatboard") is insulated to improve energy efficiency and provide a stable surface for items.

Advantages

Abundant Natural Light:
The multi-pane design captures light from various angles, ideal for growing herbs, flowers, or small indoor plants.

Added Space:
Extends the usable area of the room, offering a small ledge or shelf for practical or decorative purposes.

Architectural Appeal:
Adds a distinctive feature to both the interior and exterior of the home.

Energy Efficiency:
Modern garden windows use insulated glass and energy-efficient materials to help minimize heat loss or gain.

Common Uses

Kitchens:
Commonly placed above kitchen sinks, providing convenient access to sunlight and space for potted herbs or decorations.

Breakfast Nooks or Living Areas:
Adds charm and a sense of openness to cozy interior spaces.





Circle Top Windows

Definition

Circle top windows — also known as arch windows or half-moon windows — feature a gracefully curved or semi-circular design. They are typically installed above standard rectangular windows or doors to enhance architectural beauty and allow additional natural light into a room.

Features

Curved or Semi-Circular Shape:
Designed with an elegant arch, ranging from a perfect half-circle to a soft elliptical curve.

Fixed Pane:
These windows are non-operable, meaning they remain stationary and serve primarily for aesthetics and lighting.

Decorative Element:
Often incorporated as an architectural accent, circle top windows add character and sophistication to a building's design.

Advantages

Enhanced Natural Light:
Positioned high on walls, circle top windows allow sunlight to filter in, brightening spaces without compromising privacy.

Architectural Charm:
The curved design adds a distinctive focal point that elevates the look of both traditional and modern homes.

Design Versatility:
Easily paired with other window styles — such as casement, picture, or double-hung windows — to create a seamless and elegant window composition.

Common Uses

Residential Applications:
Frequently used in living rooms, foyers, and bedrooms to introduce natural light and decorative appeal.

Above Doors or Rectangular Windows:
Commonly placed above entry doors or picture windows, emphasizing height and enhancing the home's exterior design.
